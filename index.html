Introducing Mark Wieting

I am an investigative journalist and documentary director with over 25 years of experience in Spanish television, working across Antena 3, La Sexta, Tele 5, TVE, and regional channels as reporter, scriptwriter, and director. My work spans landmark true crime series — including ¿Dónde está Marta? (Netflix) and Anglés: Historia de una fuga (Atresplayer/Netflix) — as well as the recently completed show Territorio Pampliega for Cuatro. Based in Madrid, I am currently developing new documentary projects at Factoría Henneo, always in pursuit of stories that demand rigorous investigation and powerful narrative.

Three Stories That Inspire My Journalism

These investigations represent the depth, courage, and public impact I aspire to in my own work.

The New York Times

NYT Visual Investigations: War Crimes & Accountability

The Times's Visual Investigations unit uses open-source imagery, satellite data, and forensic video analysis to hold the powerful accountable — from Gaza to Ukraine. For me, this represents journalism's highest purpose: making the invisible visible with irrefutable evidence. As a documentary maker, their methodology of building a visual argument frame by frame is directly applicable to the screen.

The Guardian

How Mossad Allegedly Threatened the ICC Prosecutor

The Guardian revealed how Israeli intelligence allegedly intimidated the ICC's chief prosecutor to derail war crimes investigations — a cross-border scoop that triggered global diplomatic consequences. This story is a masterclass in source-driven, high-risk journalism: the kind of investigation that changes the course of events and demonstrates why a free press is essential to international justice.

El País (English)

Spain's Biggest Corruption Case: Dozens Sentenced

El País covered the sentencing of dozens of officials in the largest corruption trial in Spain's democratic history — exposing a vast network of bribes, slush funds, and political cover-ups stretching across decades. For me, this story is proof that Spanish-language newsrooms can lead global accountability journalism, and it speaks directly to the kind of investigative work I pursue: stories rooted in public trust and the consequences of its betrayal.
