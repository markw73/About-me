Spain’s youth unemployment remains among Europe’s highest

New Eurostat and INE data show that Spain’s labour market is improving, but youth unemployment remains stubbornly high compared with the rest of the EU.

By Mark Wieting

Spain’s youth unemployment remains one of the highest in Europe, according to the latest Eurostat figures for December 2025. The youth unemployment rate for people aged 15 to 24 stood at 23.4%, well above the EU average of 14.7% and the euro area average of 14.3%.

That gap matters because it shows that Spain’s labour market recovery is not reaching younger workers at the same pace as the rest of the population. Even as the country’s overall unemployment rate has improved, many young people are still struggling to get a stable first job.

“Spain’s youth unemployment is still high because the labour market creates too many unstable entry-level jobs,” said an economist at BBVA Research.

Official data from Spain’s National Statistics Institute also show how persistent the problem remains. In the first quarter of 2026, Spain’s unemployment rate rose to 10.83%, while the number of unemployed people increased to 2,708,600.

The youth figure should be read carefully: Eurostat measures youth unemployment as a share of the labour force aged 15 to 24, not as a share of the total youth population. That means the rate captures young people actively seeking work, rather than all people in that age group.

In the Eurostat table, Germany’s youth unemployment rate was 6.8%, compared with 18.1% in France, 20.5% in Italy and 13.0% in Greece. Spain still stands out as one of the countries where the problem is most severe.

The story is simple: Spain is doing better on headline unemployment, but the youngest workers remain stuck at a much higher level than their peers in most of Europe.
